## Krylov Subspace

> [!definition|] Krylov Sequence
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. The Krylov sequence generated by $A$ and $b$ is $b, Ab, A^2b, A^3b, \ldots$.

> [!definition|] Krylov Subspace
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. The $k$th Krylov subspace generated by $A$ and $b$ is $\mathcal{K}_k = \langle b, Ab, \ldots, A^{k-1}b \rangle \subseteq \mathbb{C}^{n}$.

> [!definition|] Krylov Matrix
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. The $n \times k$ Krylov matrix is $K_k = \begin{pmatrix} b & Ab & \cdots & A^{k-1}b \end{pmatrix}$.

> [!definition|] Ritz Value
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $H_k \in \mathbb{C}^{k, k}$. $H_k$ is the Hessenberg matrix obtained from the Arnoldi iteration at step $k$. The Ritz values (or Arnoldi eigenvalue estimates) of $A$ at step $k$ are the eigenvalues of $H_k$.

## Arnoldi Iteration

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $Q_k \in \mathbb{C}^{n, k}$. $Q_k$ is the matrix whose columns are the first $k$ orthonormal vectors generated by the Arnoldi iteration. $AQ_k = Q_{k+1}\tilde{H}_k$, where $\tilde{H}_k \in \mathbb{C}^{k+1, k}$ is the $(k+1) \times k$ upper-left section of the Hessenberg matrix.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $Q_k \in \mathbb{C}^{n, k}$. The matrices $Q_k$ generated by the Arnoldi iteration are reduced QR factors of the Krylov matrix: $K_k = Q_kR_k$. The Hessenberg matrices $H_k$ are the corresponding projections $H_k = Q_k^{*}AQ_k$, and the successive iterates are related by $AQ_k = Q_{k+1}\tilde{H}_k$.

## How Arnoldi Locates Eigenvalues

> [!definition|] Arnoldi Approximation Problem
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $P^k = \{$monic polynomials of degree $k\}$. The Arnoldi approximation problem at step $k$ is to find $p^k \in P^k$ s.t. $\|p^k(A)b\| = \underset{p \in P^k}{\min} \|p(A)b\|$.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $H_k \in \mathbb{C}^{k, k}$. $H_k$ is the Hessenberg matrix obtained from the Arnoldi iteration at step $k$. Assume the Krylov matrix $K_k$ is of full rank. The Arnoldi approximation problem has a unique solution $p^k$, namely the characteristic polynomial of $H_k$.

`\begin{proof}`
Let $Q = \begin{pmatrix} Q_k & U \end{pmatrix}$ and $H = \begin{pmatrix} H_k & X_1 \\ X_2 & X_3 \end{pmatrix}$ from the factorization $A = QHQ^{*}$. The orthogonality condition $Q_k^{*}p^k(A)b = 0$ amounts to the first $k$ entries of $p^k(H)e_1$ being zero. By the structure of $H$, these are the first $k$ entries of $p^k(H_k)e_1$. By the Cayley–Hamilton theorem, these are zero iff $p^k$ is the characteristic polynomial of $H_k$.
`\end{proof}`

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $\{\theta_j\}$ be the Ritz values at step $k$ of the Arnoldi iteration.
> Translation-invariance: if $A$ is changed to $A + \sigma I$ for some $\sigma \in \mathbb{C}$ and $b$ is left unchanged, the Ritz values change to $\{\theta_j + \sigma\}$.
> Scale-invariance: if $A$ is changed to $\sigma A$ for some $\sigma \in \mathbb{C}$ and $b$ is left unchanged, the Ritz values change to $\{\sigma\theta_j\}$.
> Invariance under unitary similarity: if $A$ is changed to $UAU^{*}$ for some unitary matrix $U$ and $b$ is changed to $Ub$, the Ritz values $\{\theta_j\}$ do not change.

## GMRES

> [!definition|] GMRES
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $x_k \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. GMRES (Generalized Minimal Residuals) is the algorithm that at step $k$ finds $x_k \in \mathcal{K}_k$ that minimizes $\|b - Ax_k\|$ over all $x_k \in \mathcal{K}_k$.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. Assume $A$ is nonsingular. GMRES terminates in at most $n$ steps with the exact solution $x = A^{-1}b$.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $x_k \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. The GMRES residual at step $k$ satisfies $\frac{\|r_k\|}{\|b\|} \le \underset{p \in P_k}{\inf} \|p(A)\|$, where $P_k$ is the set of polynomials $p$ of degree at most $k$ with $p(0) = 1$ and $r_k = b - Ax_k$.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $\{r_k\}$ be the GMRES residuals.
> Scale-invariance: if $A$ is changed to $\sigma A$ for some $\sigma \in \mathbb{C}$ and $b$ is changed to $\sigma b$, the residuals change to $\{\sigma r_k\}$.
> Invariance under unitary similarity: if $A$ is changed to $UAU^{*}$ for some unitary matrix $U$ and $b$ is changed to $Ub$, the residuals change to $\{Ur_k\}$.
> GMRES is not invariant under translation.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. Assume $A$ is diagonalizable with $A = X\Lambda X^{-1}$. The GMRES residual at step $k$ satisfies $\frac{\|r_k\|}{\|b\|} \le \kappa(X) \underset{p \in P_k}{\inf} \underset{\lambda \in \Lambda(A)}{\max} |p(\lambda)|$.

`\begin{proof}`
For any $p \in P_k$, $\|p(A)\| = \|Xp(\Lambda)X^{-1}\| \le \|X\|\|p(\Lambda)\|\|X^{-1}\| = \kappa(X)\|p(\Lambda)\|$. Since $\Lambda$ is diagonal, $\|p(\Lambda)\| = \underset{\lambda \in \Lambda(A)}{\max} |p(\lambda)|$. Combining with $\frac{\|r_k\|}{\|b\|} \le \underset{p \in P_k}{\inf} \|p(A)\|$ gives the result.
`\end{proof}`

## Lanczos Iteration

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $Q_k \in \mathbb{C}^{n, k}$. Assume $A = A^{*}$. The Arnoldi iteration applied to $A$ reduces to a three-term recurrence: $AQ_k = Q_kT_k + \beta_kq_{k+1}e_k^{*}$, where $T_k = Q_k^{*}AQ_k \in \mathbb{R}^{k, k}$ is real symmetric tridiagonal.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, $Q_k \in \mathbb{C}^{n, k}$, and $T_k \in \mathbb{R}^{k, k}$. Assume $A = A^{*}$. $Q_k$ and $T_k$ are generated by the Lanczos iteration. The matrices $Q_k$ are reduced QR factors of the Krylov matrix: $K_k = Q_kR_k$. The tridiagonal matrices $T_k$ are the corresponding projections: $T_k = Q_k^{*}AQ_k$. The successive iterates are related by $AQ_k = Q_{k+1}\tilde{T}_k$. As long as the Krylov matrix $K_k$ is of full rank, the characteristic polynomial of $T_k$ is the unique polynomial $p^k \in P^k$ that solves the Arnoldi/Lanczos approximation problem, i.e., $\|p^k(A)b\| = \underset{p \in P^k}{\min} \|p(A)b\|$.

> [!proposition|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$. Assume $A = A^{*}$. Each step of the Lanczos iteration requires one matrix-vector multiplication and $O(n)$ additional operations.

## From Lanczos to Gauss Quadrature

> [!definition|] Jacobi Matrix
> Let $T_k \in \mathbb{R}^{k, k}$. $T_k$ is a symmetric tridiagonal matrix with entries $\alpha_j$ on the diagonal and $\beta_j$ on the sub- and superdiagonals, arising from the Lanczos iteration or the three-term recurrence for orthogonal polynomials. In the context of orthogonal polynomials, $T_k$ is called a Jacobi matrix.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $\{q_k(x)\}$ be the sequence of orthogonal polynomials generated by the Lanczos three-term recurrence, and $\{T_k\}$ be the associated sequence of Jacobi matrices. Let $p^k$ be the characteristic polynomial of $T_k$. For $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$, $p^k(x) = C_kq_{k+1}(x)$, where $C_k$ is a constant. The zeros of $q_{k+1}(x)$ are the eigenvalues of $T_k$. These $k$ zeros are distinct and lie in the open interval $(-1, 1)$.

`\begin{proof}`
By Theorem 36.1, $p^k$ is the solution to the orthogonal polynomials approximation problem: $\|p^k(x)\| = \underset{p \in P^k}{\min} \|p(x)\|$. Any $p \in P^k$ can be written $p(x) = Cq_{k+1}(x) + Q_ky$, where $C$ is the inverse of the leading coefficient of $q_{k+1}(x)$. Since $\{q_j(x)\}$ are orthogonal, $\|p\| = (C^2 + \|y\|^2)^{1/2}$. The minimum is achieved by setting $y = 0$, so $p^k(x) = C_kq_{k+1}(x)$.
`\end{proof}`

> [!theorem|]
> Let $\{x_j\}$ be an arbitrary set of $k$ distinct points in $[-1, 1]$. There is a unique choice of weights $\{w_j\}$ with the property that the quadrature formula $I_k(f) = \sum_{j=1}^{k} w_jf(x_j)$ has order of accuracy at least $k - 1$, in the sense that it is exact if $f(x)$ is any polynomial of degree $\le k - 1$.

> [!theorem|]
> The $k$-point Gauss–Legendre quadrature formula has order of accuracy exactly $2k - 1$, and no quadrature formula $I_k(f) = \sum_{j=1}^{k} w_jf(x_j)$ has order of accuracy higher.

`\begin{proof}`
Given any set of distinct points $\{x_j\}$, let $f(x) = \prod_{j=1}^{k}(x - x_j)^2$ of degree $2k$. Then $I(f) > 0$ but $I_k(f) = 0$ since $f(x_j) = 0$ for each node $x_j$. Thus the quadrature formula is not exact for polynomials of degree $2k$. For the Gauss–Legendre nodes $\{x_j\}$ (zeros of $q_{k+1}$), suppose $f(x)$ is a polynomial of degree $\le 2k - 1$. Factor $f(x) = g(x)q_{k+1}(x) + r(x)$, where $\deg(g) \le k - 1$ and $\deg(r) \le k - 1$. Since $q_{k+1}$ is orthogonal to all polynomials of lower degree, $I(gq_{k+1}) = 0$, so $I(f) = I(r)$. Since $g(x_j)q_{k+1}(x_j) = 0$ for each node, $I_k(f) = I_k(r)$. Since $r$ has degree $\le k - 1$, $I(r) = I_k(r)$ by Theorem 37.2. Combining gives $I(f) = I_k(f)$.
`\end{proof}`

> [!theorem|]
> Let $T_k \in \mathbb{R}^{k, k}$ be the Jacobi matrix with entries $\beta_1, \ldots, \beta_{k-1}$ given by the Lanczos recurrence. Let $T_k = VDV^{\top}$ with $V = \begin{pmatrix} |v_1| & \cdots & |v_k| \end{pmatrix}$ and $D = \mathrm{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k)$. The nodes and weights of the Gauss–Legendre quadrature formula are $x_j = \lambda_j$ and $w_j = 2(v_j)_1^2$, $j = 1, \ldots, k$.

## Conjugate Gradients

> [!definition|] Conjugate Gradient Method
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, and $x_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite. The conjugate gradient method at step $k$ finds $x_k \in \mathcal{K}_k$ that minimizes $\|x - x_k\|_A = \sqrt{(x - x_k)^{\top}A(x - x_k)}$ over all $x_k \in \mathcal{K}_k$, where $x = A^{-1}b$.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, $x_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, $r_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, and $p_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite. $r_k$ and $p_k$ are the residuals and search directions of Algorithm 38.1. As long as the iteration has not yet converged (i.e., $r_{k-1} \neq 0$), $\mathcal{K}_k = \langle x_1, \ldots, x_k \rangle = \langle p_0, \ldots, p_{k-1} \rangle = \langle r_0, \ldots, r_{k-1} \rangle = \langle b, Ab, \ldots, A^{k-1}b \rangle$. The residuals are orthogonal: $r_k^{\top}r_j = 0$ for $j < k$. The search directions are $A$-conjugate: $p_k^{\top}Ap_j = 0$ for $j < k$.

`\begin{proof}`
By induction on $k$. From $x_0 = 0$ and $x_k = x_{k-1} + \alpha_kp_{k-1}$, we have $x_k \in \langle p_0, \ldots, p_{k-1} \rangle$. From $p_k = r_k + \beta_kp_{k-1}$, $\langle p_0, \ldots, p_{k-1} \rangle = \langle r_0, \ldots, r_{k-1} \rangle$. From $r_k = r_{k-1} - \alpha_kAp_{k-1}$, $\langle r_0, \ldots, r_{k-1} \rangle = \langle b, Ab, \ldots, A^{k-1}b \rangle$. This establishes (38.3).
For (38.4), apply $r_k = r_{k-1} - \alpha_kAp_{k-1}$ to compute $r_k^{\top}r_j = r_{k-1}^{\top}r_j - \alpha_kp_{k-1}^{\top}Ar_j$. If $j < k - 1$, both terms are zero by induction. If $j = k - 1$, the first term is $r_{k-1}^{\top}r_{k-1}$ and the second is $\alpha_k p_{k-1}^{\top}Ar_{k-1}$. Since $\alpha_k = (r_{k-1}^{\top}r_{k-1})/(p_{k-1}^{\top}Ap_{k-1})$ and $Ar_{k-1} \in \langle r_0, \ldots, r_k \rangle$, the induction hypothesis and the definition of $\alpha_k$ give $r_k^{\top}r_{k-1} = 0$.
For (38.5), apply $p_k = r_k + \beta_kp_{k-1}$ to compute $p_k^{\top}Ap_j = r_k^{\top}Ap_j + \beta_kp_{k-1}^{\top}Ap_j$. If $j < k - 1$, both terms are zero by induction since $Ap_j$ produces vectors in $\langle r_0, \ldots, r_{j+1} \rangle$ and $r_k$ is orthogonal to all of these. If $j = k - 1$, $\beta_k = (r_k^{\top}r_k)/(r_{k-1}^{\top}r_{k-1})$ ensures $p_k^{\top}Ap_{k-1} = 0$.
`\end{proof}`

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, $x_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, and $e_k = x - x_k$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite. $x = A^{-1}b$. If the CG iteration has not already converged (i.e., $r_{k-1} \neq 0$), $x_k$ is the unique point in $\mathcal{K}_k$ that minimizes $\|e_k\|_A$. The convergence is monotonic: $\|e_k\|_A \le \|e_{k-1}\|_A$, and $e_k = 0$ is achieved for some $k \le n$.

`\begin{proof}`
By Theorem 38.1, $x_k \in \mathcal{K}_k$. For any $x = x_k - \Delta x \in \mathcal{K}_k$, $\|e\|_A^2 = (e_k + \Delta x)^{\top}A(e_k + \Delta x) = e_k^{\top}Ae_k + (\Delta x)^{\top}A(\Delta x) + 2e_k^{\top}A(\Delta x)$. The last term is $2r_k^{\top}(\Delta x)$, an inner product of $r_k$ with a vector in $\mathcal{K}_k$. By orthogonality (Theorem 38.1), this is zero. Since $A$ is positive definite, $(\Delta x)^{\top}A(\Delta x) \ge 0$ with equality iff $\Delta x = 0$. Thus $\|e\|_A$ is minimal iff $\Delta x = 0$, i.e., $x = x_k$.
`\end{proof}`

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, $x = A^{-1}b$, and $x_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite. Assume $A$ has eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \ge \lambda_2 \ge \cdots \ge \lambda_n > 0$. The CG error at step $k$ satisfies $\frac{\|x - x_k\|_A}{\|x - x_0\|_A} \le \underset{p \in P_k}{\inf} \underset{1 \le j \le n}{\max} |p(\lambda_j)|$, where $P_k$ is the set of polynomials $p$ of degree at most $k$ with $p(0) = 1$.

`\begin{proof}`
By Theorem 38.2, $e_k = p_k(A)e_0$ for some $p_k \in P_k$. If $e_0 = \sum_{j=1}^{n} a_jv_j$ in orthonormal eigenvectors, $\|e_0\|_A^2 = \sum a_j^2\lambda_j$ and $\|p(A)e_0\|_A^2 = \sum a_j^2\lambda_j(p(\lambda_j))^2$. These imply $\|p(A)e_0\|_A^2/\|e_0\|_A^2 \le \underset{\lambda \in \Lambda(A)}{\max} |p(\lambda)|^2$.
`\end{proof}`

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite. If $A$ has only $k$ distinct eigenvalues, the CG iteration converges in at most $k$ steps.

`\begin{proof}`
A polynomial $p(x) = \prod_{j=1}^{k}(1 - x/\lambda_j) \in P_k$ exists that is zero at all $k$ distinct eigenvalues $\{\lambda_j\}$. By Theorem 38.3, $\|e_k\|_A / \|e_0\|_A \le \underset{1 \le j \le n}{\max} |p(\lambda_j)| = 0$.
`\end{proof}`

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$, $x = A^{-1}b$, and $x_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite with $\kappa = \kappa(A) = \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_n}$. The CG residual at step $k$ satisfies $\frac{\|x - x_k\|_A}{\|x - x_0\|_A} \le 2\left(\frac{\sqrt{\kappa} - 1}{\sqrt{\kappa} + 1}\right)^k$.

`\begin{proof}`
The Chebyshev polynomial of degree $k$ on $[\lambda_n, \lambda_1]$ normalized to have value $1$ at $0$ gives $\underset{p \in P_k}{\min} \underset{\lambda_n \le \lambda \le \lambda_1}{\max} |p(\lambda)| = \frac{1}{T_k\left(\frac{\lambda_1 + \lambda_n}{\lambda_1 - \lambda_n}\right)}$, where $T_k$ is the Chebyshev polynomial of degree $k$. Using $T_k(x) \ge \frac{1}{2}\left(x + \sqrt{x^2 - 1}\right)^k$ and substituting $x = \frac{\kappa + 1}{\kappa - 1}$ gives the result.
`\end{proof}`

> [!proposition|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite. Each step of the conjugate gradient iteration requires one matrix-vector multiplication and $O(n)$ additional operations.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{R}^{n}$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite. Assume $A$ is nonsingular. The conjugate gradient method terminates in at most $n$ steps with the exact solution $x = A^{-1}b$.

## Biorthogonalization Methods

> [!definition|] Biorthogonal Vectors
> Let $V \in \mathbb{C}^{n, k}$ and $W \in \mathbb{C}^{n, k}$. The columns $\{v_j\}$ of $V$ and $\{w_j\}$ of $W$ are biorthogonal if $w_i^{*}v_j = \delta_{ij}$ for $1 \le i, j \le k$.

> [!definition|] Tridiagonal Biorthogonalization
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $V \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$. $V$ is nonsingular. A tridiagonal biorthogonalization of $A$ is a factorization $A = VTV^{-1}$, where $T$ is tridiagonal. Setting $W = V^{-*}$, the columns of $V$ and $W$ are biorthogonal.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $V_k \in \mathbb{C}^{n, k}$, $W_k \in \mathbb{C}^{n, k}$, and $T_k \in \mathbb{C}^{k, k}$. The biorthogonalization process generates biorthogonal vectors $\{v_j\}$ and $\{w_j\}$ satisfying $AV_k = V_{k+1}\tilde{T}_k$, $A^{*}W_k = W_{k+1}\tilde{S}_k$, $T_k = S_k^{*} = W_k^{*}AV_k$, where $\tilde{T}_k$ and $\tilde{S}_k$ are $(k+1) \times k$ tridiagonal matrices. The vectors lie in Krylov subspaces: $v_k \in \langle v_1, Av_1, \ldots, A^{k-1}v_1 \rangle$ and $w_k \in \langle w_1, A^{*}w_1, \ldots, (A^{*})^{k-1}w_1 \rangle$.

> [!definition|] CGN (CG Applied to Normal Equations)
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. $A$ is nonsingular. CGN solves $Ax = b$ by applying the conjugate gradient method to the normal equations $A^{*}Ax = A^{*}b$.

> [!theorem|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $\kappa = \kappa(A)$. The CGN iteration applied to $Ax = b$ satisfies $\frac{\|r_k\|_2}{\|r_0\|_2} \le 2\left(\frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}\right)^k$, where $r_k = b - Ax_k$.

> [!definition|] BCG (Biconjugate Gradients)
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$, $b \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$, and $w_1 \in \mathbb{C}^{n}$. $A$ is nonsingular. BCG solves $Ax = b$ by picking $x_k \in \mathcal{K}_k = \langle b, Ab, \ldots, A^{k-1}b \rangle$ s.t. $r_k \perp \langle w_1, A^{*}w_1, \ldots, (A^{*})^{k-1}w_1 \rangle$, where $w_1$ is an arbitrary vector satisfying $w_1^{*}v_1 = 1$.

## Preconditioning

> [!definition|] Preconditioner
> Let $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$ and $M \in \mathbb{C}^{n, n}$. $M$ is nonsingular. A preconditioner for $A$ is a matrix $M$ s.t. the system $M^{-1}Ax = M^{-1}b$ (left preconditioning) or $AM^{-1}y = b$ with $x = M^{-1}y$ (right preconditioning) is better conditioned or has more clustered eigenvalues than the original system $Ax = b$.

> [!proposition|]
> Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$ and $M \in \mathbb{R}^{n, n}$. $A = A^{\top}$. $A$ is positive definite. $M = C^{\top}C$ for some nonsingular $C$. The preconditioned conjugate gradient method applied to $C^{-\top}AC^{-1}$ converges at a rate determined by $\kappa(C^{-\top}AC^{-1}) = \kappa(M^{-1}A)$, and the convergence bound becomes $\frac{\|x - x_k\|_A}{\|x - x_0\|_A} \le 2\left(\frac{\sqrt{\kappa(M^{-1}A)} - 1}{\sqrt{\kappa(M^{-1}A)} + 1}\right)^k$.
